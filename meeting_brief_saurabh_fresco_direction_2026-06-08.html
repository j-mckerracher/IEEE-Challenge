Meeting Brief: FRESCO Research Direction After Cross-Cluster Transfer Results

Date: 2026-06-08

Purpose

This brief is for discussing the strategic research direction for FRESCO after the current cross-cluster transfer findings.

Core Message

The main value of the current results is not simply that transfer failed.

Schema harmonization alone is insufficient for defensible cross-cluster analysis; provenance and measurement-semantic standards are likely a necessary precondition.

What the Results Mean

The experiments suggest that cross-cluster predictive transfer is unreliable under ordinary heterogeneous HPC data collection practices. The problem appears structural, not just a weak-model issue:

  • different clusters measure resources differently,
  • similar features may not mean the same thing across sites,
  • hardware and workload differences create additional shift,
  • temporal differences add further instability.
Without explicit provenance and comparable measurement semantics, cross-cluster claims may be uninterpretable or misleading.

Important Nuance: What Did Work

Pooled models are not the same as transfer.

Earlier FRESCO work showed that pooled multi-cluster models can perform reasonably when the target cluster is already represented in training. That is useful, but it does not solve the harder question of zero-shot or near-zero-shot deployment to a target cluster with no target-domain training data.

So the correct message is not “all cross-cluster use fails equally.” The stronger claim is: models do not reliably transfer to a target cluster when that target is not represented in training and measurement semantics differ.

Why This Matters to the HPC Community

If we eventually want to tell the HPC community that provenance standards are needed for cross-cluster analysis, they will ask: Why?

The answer should be: because we now have empirical evidence that, without those standards, cross-cluster predictive analysis breaks down or becomes unstable.

Small Example of Semantic Non-Equivalence

Two clusters may both expose something called memory usage, but the metric may not mean the same thing.

  • Cluster A: memory accounting includes page cache
  • Cluster B: memory accounting excludes page cache or tracks RSS instead

In that case, the shared column name does not imply a shared operational quantity. A model trained on Cluster A can learn relationships tied to A’s accounting rules and then fail when deployed on Cluster B.

Other semantic mismatches can include different sampling intervals, aggregation rules, cache policies, and telemetry sources.

Strategic Options

Option 1: Publish the standards-motivation argument first

This is the preferred next step.

  • Use the current evidence to argue that provenance standards are necessary for defensible cross-cluster analysis
  • Frame it not as “standards would be nice,” but as “without provenance standards, cross-cluster claims are not reliably interpretable”

Option 2: Treat cross-cluster comparability as the next open research question

This is the next direction to discuss after the standards argument is established.

  • Which provenance elements matter most?
  • Which measurement mismatches are fatal?
  • Are some targets more transferable than others?
  • Are there constrained settings where comparability is achievable?

Option 3: Redesign or reconstruct the dataset around comparability goals

This comes later, after the evidentiary and methodological case is stronger.

Recommended Sequence

  1. Publish the argument that provenance standards are necessary
  2. Investigate cross-cluster comparability as an open methodological question
  3. Only later consider dataset redesign / reconstruction for comparability

What I Want to Convey in the Meeting

I do not think the most valuable immediate next step is trying harder to force cross-cluster prediction to work.

Publish the evidence-based case that provenance and measurement-semantic standards are necessary if the community wants defensible cross-cluster analysis at all.

Then ask:

Under what conditions could cross-cluster analysis actually become possible?

Bottom Line

Use the present findings to publish the necessity of provenance standards first, then pursue cross-cluster comparability as an open research question, and only later move toward a redesigned dataset or collection strategy.
